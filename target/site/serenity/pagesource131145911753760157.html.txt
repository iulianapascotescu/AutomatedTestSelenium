you’ll probably get 20 different answers. That’s the beauty of a great song: It has the power to move you on a personal level, which is far more important than what anyone else thinks.</p><p>However, in an effort to create a list of the all-time greatest songs, we considered the views of professional music critics and fans alike, via Rolling Stone’s <a href="https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-lists/500-greatest-songs-of-all-time-151127/">500 Greatest Songs of All Time</a> and Ranker’s <a href="https://www.ranker.com/crowdranked-list/best-songs-of-all-time-ranked" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">The Best Songs of All Time</a>.</p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_1"><h2 id="header-slide-1">50. ‘Gimme Shelter’ — The Rolling Stones</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-959631718-727x500.jpg" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-959631718-727x500.jpg" class="lazyloaded" data-was-processed="true"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-959631718-727x500.jpg'/></noscript></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-1"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">For a song that <a href="https://books.google.co.uk/books?id=GjQDAwAAQBAJ&amp;pg=PT179&amp;lpg=PT179&amp;dq=keith+richards+wrote+gimme+shelter+in+20+minutes&amp;source=bl&amp;ots=8f_ZxZiEoR&amp;sig=ACfU3U3FV4w1Nez7vDUz-yoBq04Z-fC4DA&amp;hl=en&amp;sa=X&amp;ved=2ahUKEwjJsf2yhYvnAhXai1wKHQeUDHoQ6AEwGnoECA0QAQ#v=onepage&amp;q=keith%20richards%20wrote%20gimme%20shelter%20in%2020%20minutes&amp;f=false" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Keith Richards wrote in 20 minutes</a>, “Gimme Shelter” made a big impression. The opening track to the 1969 Rolling Stones album “Let It Bleed,” it was never released as a single but has been included in many compilation records and has been a staple of the band’s live gigs. During their 50th anniversary tour in 2012, the Rolling Stones sang this song with <a href="https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/rolling-stones-cap-anniversary-tour-with-an-all-star-blowout-in-newark-97981/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Lady Gaga</a>, <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_67x-JuTh_g" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Mary J. Blige</a> and <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JkbcuKxqmAM" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Florence Welch</a>.</span></p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_2"><h2 id="header-slide-2">49. ‘One’ — U2</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/12/476305313_u2-band-750x500.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/12/476305313_u2-band-750x500.jpg'/></noscript></figure></div><br><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The third track on U2’s 1991 album “Achtung Baby,” “One” was actually a spin-off from their second single “Mysterious Ways.” According to <a href="https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-lists/500-greatest-songs-of-all-time-151127/u2-one-70151/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Rolling Stone</a>, the Edge suggested two ideas for the bridge and Bono liked one of them so much, he wrote a whole new set of lyrics. “One” may be a wedding favorite, but that wasn’t what the band had in mind. </span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">“People have told me they play it at their wedding,” the Edge said. “And I think, ‘Have you listened to the lyrics? It’s not that kind of a song.'”</span></p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_3"><h2 id="header-slide-3">48. ‘No Woman, No Cry’ — Bob Marley</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/71IJx0YvW1L._SS500_-500x500.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/71IJx0YvW1L._SS500_-500x500.jpg'/></noscript></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-3"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The best-known version of “No Woman, No Cry” isn’t the original version (on the 1974 studio album “Natty Dread”); it’s the version on the following year’s “Live!” — recorded at the Lyceum Theatre in London on July 17, 1975, as part of Marley’s Natty Dread Tour.&nbsp;</span></p><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">This song didn’t just change Marley’s life; he gave a songwriting credit to his <a href="https://www.independent.co.uk/arts-entertainment/music/news/bob-marley-no-woman-no-cry-royalties-dispute-opens-at-high-court-9364763.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">childhood friend Vincent “Tata” Ford</a>, which helped Ford keep his Kingston soup <a href="https://www.dontwasteyourmoney.com/kitchen/">kitchen</a> afloat.</span></p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_4"><h2 id="header-slide-4">47. ‘You’ve Lost That Lovin’ Feeling’ — The Righteous Brothers</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/1841066_bill-medley-righteous-707x500.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/1841066_bill-medley-righteous-707x500.jpg'/></noscript><figcaption><cite class="op-vertical-below credits-overlay-new"><a href="http://www.gettyimages.com/license/1841066">Getty Images | Frank Micelotta</a></cite></figcaption></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-4"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">“You’ve Lost That Lovin’ Feeling” was first recorded by The Righteous Brothers in 1964 and reached the top of the charts in both the U.S. and the U.K. It was also the <a href="https://www.americanradiohistory.com/Archive-Billboard/60s/1965/Billboard-1965-International-Record-&amp;-Talent.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">fifth-bestselling song in the U.S. in 1965</a>. The song has been covered by a number of other artists, including Dionne Warwick and Hall and Oates, but no version has the impact of Bill Medley’s deep vocal, without instruments, in the intro: “You never close your eyes anymore when I kiss your lips.”</span></p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_5"><h2 id="header-slide-5">46. ‘Sympathy For The Devil’ — The Rolling Stones</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/GettyImages-459397604-750x500.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/GettyImages-459397604-750x500.jpg'/></noscript></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-5"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p>The Rolling Stones are no strangers to controversy when it comes to their music, and their 1968 release “Sympathy for the Devil,” from the album “Beggars Banquet,” was no different. The song caused a stir among some religious groups, who feared that the <a href="https://www.independent.co.uk/arts-entertainment/music/features/rolling-stones-sympathy-for-the-devil-mick-jagger-anniversary-satanism-a8668551.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Stones were devil-worshippers</a>. However, in a 1995 interview with <a href="https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/mick-jagger-remembers-92946/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Rolling Stone</a>, Mick Jagger said the song was an idea he got from French writing.</p><p>“I just took a couple of lines and expanded on it,” he said. “I wrote it as sort of like a Bob Dylan song.”</p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_6"><h2 id="header-slide-6">45. ‘I Walk The Line’ — Johnny Cash</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/10/GettyImages-74256740-702x500.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/10/GettyImages-74256740-702x500.jpg'/></noscript></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-6"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p>While he was <a href="http://npr.org/2000/12/23/1115971/npr-100-i-walk-the-line?t=1579278518655" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">stationed in Germany with the Air Force</a>, Johnny Cash started working on “I Walk the Line.” It was many years later, in 1956, when he decided to record it, but realized that the original tape was damaged. However, this ended up being a bonus; he embraced the unique sound and added even more interest by wrapping a piece of wax paper around the strings of his <a href="https://www.dontwasteyourmoney.com/best-acoustic-guitar/">guitar</a>. data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-524362698-750x484.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-524362698-750x484.jpg'/></noscript><figcaption><cite class="op-vertical-below credits-overlay-new"><a href="">Getty Images</a></cite></figcaption></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-39"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p>Led Zeppelin’s epic 1971 release “Stairway to Heaven” is a big hit within the Ranker community, coming in at their seventh best song of all time.</p><p>Planet Rock readers voted it the <a href="https://www.planetrock.com/music/backstage/the-rock-lists/the-greatest-ever-rock-song/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">greatest rock song of all time</a>, giving it more than twice the amount of votes of its closest rival, Queen’s “Bohemian Rhapsody,” and it has also been voted the <a href="https://www.gigwise.com/news/55896/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">U.K.’s favorite rock anthem</a>.</p><p>Despite the fact that it was never commercially released as a single in the U.S., it was the most requested song on FM radio stations in the 1970s, proving the power of the band’s growing fan base.</p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_40"><h2 id="header-slide-40">11. ‘Like A Rolling Stone’ — Bob Dylan</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-107363838-730x500.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-107363838-730x500.jpg'/></noscript><figcaption><cite class="op-vertical-below credits-overlay-new"><a href="">Getty Images</a></cite></figcaption></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-40"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p>Rolling Stone ranks Bob Dylan’s 1965 release “Like a Rolling Stone” the greatest song of all time, <a href="https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-lists/500-greatest-songs-of-all-time-151127/bob-dylan-like-a-rolling-stone-2-54028/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">writing</a>, “No other pop song has so thoroughly challenged and transformed the commercial laws and artistic conventions of its time, for all time.”</p><p>A longer than average track at six minutes, 13 seconds, radio stations were initially reluctant to play it, but it nonetheless became a huge worldwide hit, reaching No. 2 on the Billboard Hot 100. It’s been covered by everyone from The Jimi Hendrix Experience to Green Day.</p><p>According to review aggregator <a href="http://www.acclaimedmusic.net/year/alltime_songs.htm" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Acclaimed Music</a>, “Like a Rolling Stone” is the statistically most acclaimed song of all time. At a 2014 auction, Dylan’s handwritten lyrics to the song <a href="https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-us-canada-28009344" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">fetched $2 million</a>, a world record for a pop-music manuscript.</p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_41"><h2 id="header-slide-41">10. ‘God Only Knows’ — The Beach Boys</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-84879733-743x500.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-84879733-743x500.jpg'/></noscript><figcaption><cite class="op-vertical-below credits-overlay-new"><a href="">Getty Images</a></cite></figcaption></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-41"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p>Voted 25th by Rolling Stone, 19th by the Ranker community, one of the <a href="http://wyep.org/blog/the-songs-that-shaped-rock-and-roll" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">500 songs that shaped rock and roll</a> by the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame and the greatest song of the 1960s by <a href="https://pitchfork.com/features/lists-and-guides/6405-the-200-greatest-songs-of-the-1960s/?page=2" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Pitchfork Media</a>, “God Only Knows” wasn’t the biggest chart hit for the Beach Boys (it was released as the B-side of “Wouldn’t It Be Nice” in the U.S.), but it remains a firm fan favorite. In fact, <a href="https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-lists/readers-poll-the-best-beach-boys-songs-of-all-time-15656/1-god-only-knows-234468/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Rolling Stone readers</a> voted it the best Beach Boys song, and even fellow ’60s creative genius Paul McCartney has said it’s his <a href="https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/beach-boys-pet-sounds-15-things-you-didnt-know-186297/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">favorite song of all time</a>.</p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_42"><h2 id="header-slide-42">9. ‘Blowin’ In The Wind’ — Bob Dylan</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-85062699-637x500.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-85062699-637x500.jpg'/></noscript><figcaption><cite class="op-vertical-below credits-overlay-new"><a href="">Getty Images</a></cite></figcaption></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-42"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p>“Blowin’ in the Wind” has been variously described as “<a href="https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-lists/500-greatest-songs-of-all-time-151127/bob-dylan-blowin-in-the-wind-53737/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Dylan’s first important composition</a>,” the most famous protest song ever, an anthem of the civil rights movement and the song Dylan is best known for, so it’s perhaps a surprise that this song didn’t chart for Bob Dylan. However, it was a massive hit for the folk band Peter, Paul and Mary in the summer of 1963, and in 1994 was inducted into the Grammy Hall of Fame.</p><p>It ranks No. 14 on Rolling Stone’s list of the 500 greatest songs of all time, and Ranker voters place it at No. 17.</p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_43"><h2 id="header-slide-43">8. ‘I Want To Hold Your Hand’ — The Beatles</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/854467_the-beatles-e1545952349307-750x500.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/854467_the-beatles-e1545952349307-750x500.jpg'/></noscript><figcaption><cite class="op-vertical-below credits-overlay-new"><a href="">Getty Images</a></cite></figcaption></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-43"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p>Another of several “best song” entries for The Beatles, “I Want to Hold Your Hand” is sixth on Ranker and 16th on Rolling Stone. Released in 1963, it was the group’s first No. 1 hit in the U.S. and stayed in the U.K. top 50 for a total of 21 weeks.</p><p>In 1980, <a href="http://www.beatlesinterviews.org/dba02with.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">John Lennon said</a> the song was written “eyeball to eyeball” with McCartney.</p><p>“I remember when we got the chord that made the song,” he recalled. “We were in Jane Asher’s house, downstairs in the cellar playing on the piano at the same time. And we had, ‘Oh you-u-u/ got that something…’ And Paul hits this chord, and I turn to him and say, ‘That’s it!’ I said, ‘Do that again!’ In those days, we really used to absolutely write like that — both playing into each other’s noses.”</p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_44"><h2 id="header-slide-44">7. ‘Johnny B. Goode’ — Chuck Berry</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/85682564_chuck-berry-725x500.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/85682564_chuck-berry-725x500.jpg'/></noscript><figcaption><cite class="op-vertical-below credits-overlay-new"><a href="">Getty Images</a></cite></figcaption></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-44"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p><a href="https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-lists/500-greatest-songs-of-all-time-151127/chuck-berry-johnny-b-goode-33695/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Rolling Stone</a> credits Chuck Berry’s 1958 hit “Johnny B. Goode” as “the first rock &amp; roll hit about rock &amp; roll stardom” and “the greatest rock &amp; roll song about the democracy of fame in pop music.”</p><p>The semi-autobiographical song about an illiterate “country boy” from the New Orleans area who plays the guitar “just like ringing a bell” peaked at No. 8 on the Billboard Hot 100. It was inducted into the Grammy Hall of Fame in 1999 for its influence as a rock-and-roll single and is No. 1 on Rolling Stone’s <a href="https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/the-new-issue-of-rolling-stone-the-100-greatest-guitar-songs-108589/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">100 Greatest Guitar Songs of All Time</a> list. It’s also a hit with Ranker voters, who put it at No. 11.</p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_45"><h2 id="header-slide-45">6. ‘Smells Like Teen Spirit’ — Nirvana</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-2225160-1-740x500.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-2225160-1-740x500.jpg'/></noscript><figcaption><cite class="op-vertical-below credits-overlay-new"><a href="">Getty Images</a></cite></figcaption></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-45"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p>The only ’90s release on the list, Nirvana’s “Smells Like Teen Spirit” became an anthem for an apathetic generation. Named after a brand of deodorant for girls, the song was the band’s biggest hit in most countries, has been certified platinum (1 million copies shipped) by the Recording Industry Association of America and sent the album “Nevermind” to the top of the charts at the start of 1992. But the song put unwanted pressure on the band.</p><p>“There are many other songs that I have written that are as good, if not better,”<a href="https://www.kerrang.com/features/kurt-on-kurt-the-nirvana-icon-in-his-own-words/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer"> claimed frontman Kurt Cobain</a>. Rolling Stone puts “Smells Like Teen Spirit” at No. 9, while Ranker voters have it at No. 13.</p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_46"><h2 id="header-slide-46">5. ‘Good Vibrations’ — The Beach Boys</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-73988774-639x500.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-73988774-639x500.jpg'/></noscript><figcaption><cite class="op-vertical-below credits-overlay-new"><a href="">Getty Images</a></cite></figcaption></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-46"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p>“Good Vibrations” was a huge hit for the Beach Boys in 1966, scoring them No. 1s in both the U.S. and the U.K. as well as many other countries.</p><p>At the time, it was the most expensive single ever recorded, with a <a href="https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/beach-boys-pet-sounds-15-things-you-didnt-know-186297/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">studio bill of $50,000</a>. Brian Wilson composed and produced the song, which was inspired by his <a href="https://www.uncut.co.uk/features/the-making-of-the-beach-boys-good-vibrations-34867" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">fascination with cosmic vibrations</a> — stemming from a moment in his childhood when his mother tried to explain why dogs barked at some people and not others.</p><p>“A dog would pick up vibrations from these people that you can’t see but you can feel. And the same thing happened with people,” Wilson said. One of his goals with the song was to create a better song than “You’ve Lost That Lovin’ Feelin,” and both Rolling Stone and Ranker believe he did it, ranking “Good Vibrations” at No. 6 and No. 8, respectively.</p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_47"><h2 id="header-slide-47">4. ‘Yesterday’ — The Beatles</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure id="attachment_53787 class=" wp-caption="" alignnone"=""><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="http://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-108731366-750x496.jpg"><noscript><img src='http://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-108731366-750x496.jpg'/></noscript><figcaption><cite class="op-vertical-below credits-overlay-new"><a href="https://www.gettyimages.com/photos/john-lennon-paul-mccartney?family=editorial&amp;phrase=john%20lennon%20paul%20mccartney&amp;sort=best#license">Getty Images</a></cite></figcaption></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-47"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p>The Beatles’ most famous ballad was voted third-best by the Ranker community and 13th by Rolling Stone. It was also ranked third on BMI’s list of the <a href="https://www.bmi.com/news/entry/232893" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Top 100 Songs of the Century</a> and was voted the best song of the 20th century in a 1999 <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/uk/1999/apr/03/vikramdodd" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">BBC Radio 2 poll</a> of music experts and listeners.</p><p>“Yesterday” only features one of the Fab Four: McCartney’s vocal over a string quartet. <a href="http://www.beatlesebooks.com/yesterday" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">McCartney described it</a> as “one of the most instinctive songs I’ve ever written.” The melody came to him in a dream while he was staying at his then-girlfriend Jane Asher’s house. But initially, the band was “a little embarrassed” about recording a song that was so far from their rock-and-roll roots.</p></div><div class="slide" id="slide_48"><h2 id="header-slide-48">3. ‘(I Can’t Get No) Satisfaction’ — The Rolling Stones</h2><div class="imgWrapper"><figure><img src="data:image/svg+xml,%3Csvg%20xmlns='http://www.w3.org/2000/svg'%20viewBox='0%200%200%200'%3E%3C/svg%3E" data-lazy-src="https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-78087164-750x499-750x499.jpg"><noscript><img src='https://3qbqmv3k0dsayahrd3pw8jru-wpengine.netdna-ssl.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/06/GettyImages-78087164-750x499-750x499.jpg'/></noscript><figcaption><cite class="op-vertical-below credits-overlay-new"><a href="">Getty Images</a></cite></figcaption></figure></div><br><div class="ad-container ad-container-responsive ad-after-img ad-after-img-48"></div><div style="clear:both;padding-top:10px"></div><p>“(I Can’t Get No) Satisfaction,” deemed by Rolling Stone to be the second-best song of all time, gave The Rolling Stones their first U.S. No. 1, and despite being initially restricted to pirate radio stations in the U.K. (due to its sexually suggestive content), it later topped the charts there, too.</p><p>The song’s unmistakable riff came to Keith Richards in a dream one night in May 1965, in his motel room in Clearwater, Florida, on the Rolling Stones’ third U.S. tour. <a href="https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-lists/500-greatest-songs-of-all-time-151127/the-rolling-stones-i-cant-get-no-satisfaction-38617/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">According to Rolling Stone</a>, “He woke up and grabbed a guitar and a cassette machine.
